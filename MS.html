MS Thesis

Amanda M Long

University of North Texas

Master of Science in Biology

Conservation, Connectivity, and Coexistence: Understanding Corridor Efficacy in Fragmented Landscapes

Background

Wildlife corridors, areas of land connecting patches of natural land cover, are frequently cited and implemented as a restorative strategy to counteract fragmentation. Quantifying the degree to which amorphous landscape configurations, tortuosity, and heterogeneity of land cover and land uses within the corridor has on functional connectedness is a crucial yet overlooked component of corridor efficacy studies. Corridor literature lacks a robust and repeatable methodology for delineating landscape elements, recognizing arbitrary edges, and identifying the start and end of ambiguous transitions between patches and corridors.

Using a set of landscapes, as part of an ongoing assessment of corridor efficacy, "Do Corridors Work?", I applied a species perspective approach to spatial modelling to quantify the landscape patterns influencing processes for small mammal focal species. My thesis research investigated the degree to which the presence of a corridor influences the structural and functional connectivity between patches connected by a corridor compared to an intact reference area and patches isolated by a matrix.

Objectives
  1. Minimize the subjectivity and variability in the delineation process of patch-corridor-matrix boundaries.
  2. Identify ambiguous transitional areas of patch-corridor interfaces.
  3. Assess the influences structural landscape configuration and composition has on functional connectivity for focal species.

Delineations

Using spatial modelling, applied existing and personally developed geospatial tools and methods to integrate field data with remotely sensed data and analyze landscape patterns.

Corridor Termini

Collected dispersal data for Felidae species listed as endangered, threatened, and vulnerable to assess the degree to which populations were isolated as a result of fragmentation
