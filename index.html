Climate Change and Disease Ecology at the Southern End of the Americas

×

As a Research Fellow with the Sub-Antarctic Biocultural Conservation Program, I actively collected field data for spatial and statistical analysis in the southernmost forests of the world (55-56Â°S), at Omora Ethnobotanical Park and Yendegaia National Park, to investigate the influence forests and wetlands have on avian populations in the context of global climate change.

Carbon analysis was conducted using forest inventory data to assess the relationship between carbon storage and avian richness using point counts. The prevalence of avian malaria was detected using DNA extraction of blood samples and PCR amplification. The research supports policymaking in the UNESCO Cape Horn Biosphere Reserve.

Land Cover and Land Use Change in North Central Texas

×

As part of an Advanced GIS course, I estimated land cover and land use change in four North Central Texas counties from 2001-2022 using the National Land Cover Database (NLCD). Over 21 years, 36% of the area changed in land cover and land use. Urbanization can be visualized as the developed areas (pink-red) sprawl across counties.

This furthered my understanding of the methods and data used to quantify land cover and land use changes as well as issues of human development, loss of natural land cover, and drought.

Skills

Attribute Extraction, Cell Statistics, Raster Calculator, Urbanization

Software

ArcGIS Pro

Data

County Boundaries, National Land Cover Database

Cumulative Human Pressure on the Environment in 2009

×

The Human Footprint Index represents cumulative human pressure on the environment from population density, built-up areas, roads/railways, crops/pastures, navigable water, and electric power infrastructure. As part of an Advanced GIS course, I used Zonal Statistics to analyze the average Human Footprint among states and within Texas and Michigan counties in 2009.

In TX, counties with a Footprint Index >60 contained Fort Worth, Dallas, Longview, and Houston, accounting for 17% of the total population.

In MI, 49% of the total population resides in the Metro Detroit Area. While 29% of land is the Upper Peninsula, it contains 3% of the total population and lowest human footprint.

Cartography, SQL, Zonal Analysis & Statistics

Software

ArcGIS Pro

Counties, 2009 Human Footprint, Open Data Network, States

Positive Tiger Responses to the Global Tiger Recovery Program

×

As an author on a publication in prep, Tiger (Panthera tigris) Endangerment: Positive Responses to Global Conservation Efforts, I reviewed pertinent literature for fecundity and survivorship data for a population viability analysis of 3 distinct tiger subpsecies: Amur (P.t. altacia), Bengal (P.t. tigris), and Sunda (P.t. sumatrae).

Tiger populations had a positive growth rate, indicating positive responses to the Global Tiger Recovery Program, an effort to double the number of wild tigers by 2022. This research supports the recent IUCN Tiger Program Phase I report that populations have increased ~40% since 2015.

Skills

Cartography, Population Viability Analysis, Spatial Analysis, SQL

Software

ArcGIS Pro, Excel, R

Data

IUCN Red List, Fecundity, Survivorship

Supervised Land Cover Classifications and Accuracy Assessments

×

Given the uncertainty of global land cover dataset accuracy among regions, I classified land covers for three landscapes in my MS thesis, Conservation, Connectivity, and Coexistence: Understanding Corridor Efficacy in Fragmented Landscapes using false composite satellite imagery.

Accuracy assessments were conducted using 500 random sample points with an overall 92%, 95%, and 95%, accuracy for Kansas, Idaho, and Czechia, respectively. Land cover layers were used to quantify the structural composition and configuration of landscapes as well as further spatial analysis modeling functional landscape connectivity.

Accuracy Assessment, Feature Extraction, Image Analysis, Supervised Classification

Digitizing Forest and Rangeland Boundaries

×

Current corridor literature lacks a unified method to delineate landscape elements for spatial models. As part of my MS thesis research, Conservation, Connectivity, and Coexistence: Understanding Corridor Efficacy in Fragmented Landscapes, I standardized the digitization of forest and rangeland boundaries by integrating GPS coordinates of small mammal traps with satellite imagery and analyzing vegetation.

Delineations were used to quantify and analyze landscape connectivity between corridor complexes (patch-corridor-patch), intact reference areas, and isolated patches.

Where Does A Corridor Start and End?

×

Understanding conservation corridor efficacy depends on identifying the start and end. For my MS thesis, Conservation, Connectivity, and Coexistence: Understanding Corridor Efficacy in Fragmented Landscapes, I used spatial and statistical analysis to identify transitional areas, where edge effects are expected to influence species movements, of 5 previously digitized corridor complexes. Patch one narrows into the corridor at 1 and 2 the corridor widens into patch two and 3 and 4.

This process raises the question, do we need to manage corridors distinctly from patches or extend management into patches to promote corridor efficacy?

Quantifying the Composition of Small Mammal Habitats Within Landscape Elements

×

Habitat quality and quantity provide species the resources to survive and reproduce. As part of my MS thesis, Conservation, Connectivity, and Coexistence: Understanding Corridor Efficacy in Fragmented Landscapes, I quantified habitat for the white-footed mouse (PELE), western harvest mouse ( REME), and yellow-necked mouse (APFL) within landscape elements.

I used previously classified land cover layers to assess the relationship between the proportion of species captured and the percent of habitat available within (a) corridor complexes, (b) corridors, (c; d) patches 1 and 2, (e) reference areas, and (f; g) isolates.

Creating Resistance Rasters to Model Landscape Connectivity

×

Resistance-based connectivity models are frequently used to assess a species response to a landscape. As part of my MS thesis, Conservation, Connectivity, and Coexistence: Understanding Corridor Efficacy in Fragmented Landscapes, I used expert literature-based models to develop resistance rasters with environmental data layers known to influence small mammal movements and distribution: land cover, roads, distance to water, and elevation.

Resistance rasters were used for cost distance and current flow models to compare the resistance values for small mammal focal species to traverse patches connected by a corridor, through intact reference areas, and between isolates through a non-habitat matrix.

Quantifying Landscape Connectivity for Small Mammals

×

As part of my MS thesis, Conservation, Connectivity, and Coexistence: Understanding Corridor Efficacy in Fragmented Landscapes, I used cost distance and current flow models to quantify landscape connectivity for small mammal focal species between: (a) corridor start and end, (b) patches connected by a corridor, (c) intact reference area, and (d) isolates separated by a matrix. The least-cost path (LCP) identified the optimal path of the lowest cost between landscape configurations whereas the current flow identified a network of all potential paths.

High current flow indicates areas with a higher degree of landscape connectivity and lower resistance.

Skills

Land Management, Small Mammal Behavior, Spatial Analysis

Software

ArcGIS Pro, Circuitscape, Excel, Julia

Data

Digitized Landscape Boundaries, Resistance Rasters
