BS Thesis

Amanda M Long

Central Michigan University

Bachelor of Science in Ecology, Evolution, and Conservation Biology

The Stepping Stones of Dispersal in Felidae

Background

Human land use and land changes fragments landscapes; reducing habitat quality, connectivity, and creating dispersal barriers. Isolated patches of habitat threatens dispersing individuals by altering wildlife behaviors and dispersal patterns. Isolation poses a risk of inbreeding and loss of genetic diversity in populations. Stepping-stones provide structural landscape connectivity and strengthen metapopulation functionality. Overall, I found the distance at which species can disperse to exceed the distance between fragmented populations, providing connectivity with stepping-stones and indicating metapopulations are able to function successfully. My analysis was restricted to species of large Felidae due to biased research and limited knowledge on smaller felids. To continue to assess and protect Felidae classified as endangered, vulnerable, and threatened, future research on understudied species is paramount to the conservation of these carnivorous mammals.

Objectives

  1. Quantify the degree to which nine species of endangered, vulnerable, and near threatened Felidae were isolated as a result of landscape fragmentation using dispersal mechanisms.
